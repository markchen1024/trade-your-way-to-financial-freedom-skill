# Chapter 13: Conclusion — Putting It All Together

## Core Idea
A complete trading system integrates *all* the parts — objectives, concept, setups, entry, stops, profit exits, and position sizing — weighted by their true importance: psychology and position sizing first, entry last. The book closes by reassembling the pieces and reaffirming that mastery is internal.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The integrated system**: Every component from Chapters 3–12 assembled into one coherent whole, designed backward from *your* objectives.
- **Correct weighting of effort**: Re-state the hierarchy — Psychology (~60%) > Position Sizing (~30%) > System/entry (~10%) — and spend effort accordingly.
- **Build-order recap (the 12 steps, condensed)**: Know yourself → set objectives → pick a time frame and concept → define stops (R) → define profit exits → measure expectancy → add position sizing → rehearse worst cases → iterate.

## Key Concepts
- **System fit**: A system only "works" if it fits the trader's objectives, beliefs, and tolerances.
- **Discipline**: The execution glue — without it, the best-designed system fails.
- **Worst-case rehearsal**: Pre-planning responses to disasters so you act instead of freeze.

## Mental Models
- Judge any new idea by *where it sits in the hierarchy*: a better entry is a 10% improvement; better discipline or sizing is transformational.
- See the finished system as a reflection of yourself — your objectives in, your behavior out.

## Anti-patterns
- **Optimizing the 10% (entry) while ignoring the 60% and 30% (psychology, sizing)** — the central mistake the whole book warns against.
- **A great design with no discipline to execute it** — the system never gets traded as intended.
- **Skipping worst-case rehearsal** — leaving crisis decisions to panic.

## Worked Example
Reassembling the coin-flip lesson into a personal system: a swing trader sets an objective (15%/yr, max 20% drawdown), picks trend following as the concept they believe, accepts a ~40% win rate, places volatility-based stops to define R, uses a trailing stop to let winners run to large R-multiples, confirms positive expectancy, and applies a 1% percent-risk position-sizing model to hit the objective. Entry is a simple breakout — deliberately *not* over-engineered, because they now know entry is the 10% pillar. Discipline and worst-case rehearsal hold it together.

## Key Takeaways
1. A real system integrates every part, weighted by true importance.
2. Spend effort where it matters: psychology and sizing, not entry.
3. Design backward from your own objectives.
4. Discipline turns a good design into actual results.
5. The Holy Grail was internal all along (Ch 1) — mastery of self completes the system.
