# Chapter 5: Selecting a Concept That Works

## Core Idea
A system needs a *concept* — a clear, objectively measurable idea about how markets move that you genuinely believe in. Tharp surveys the major trading concepts so you can pick one that fits your beliefs and temperament, not chase whichever is fashionable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Concept-driven design**: Choose a concept you *understand and believe*, because belief is what lets you trade it through drawdowns.
- **Survey of major concepts** (each with its own logic, strengths, and failure modes):
  - **Trend following** — ride sustained moves; cut losses, let profits run. Low win rate, large winners. Tharp's clear favorite for most people.
  - **Fundamental analysis** — value/supply-demand drivers; better for *what* to trade than *when*.
  - **Seasonal tendencies** — recurring calendar/weather-driven patterns (esp. commodities).
  - **Spreading** — profit from the relationship between two instruments rather than absolute direction.
  - **Arbitrage** — exploit pricing/structural discrepancies; edges are real but get competed away.
  - **Neural networks** — pattern-fitting models; high curve-fitting (degrees-of-freedom) risk.
  - **"There's an order to the universe"** — band-trading / market-order concepts.

## Key Concepts
- **Concept vs. system**: The concept is the *idea*; the system is the objective rules that implement it.
- **Belief alignment**: A concept only works for you if it matches what you believe markets do.
- **Reliability vs. reward profile**: Trend following = low reliability but big R-multiples; band/reversion concepts = higher reliability, smaller R.

## Mental Models
- Pick the concept you'd be comfortable defending and trading at 3 a.m. in a drawdown — fit beats theoretical optimality.
- Match the concept's *reliability/reward shape* to your psychology: can you stomach being "wrong" 60% of the time (trend following) for big winners?

## Anti-patterns
- **Trading a concept you don't believe**: You'll abandon it at the first drawdown.
- **Over-relying on neural networks / heavily optimized models**: Maximum exposure to the degrees-of-freedom (curve-fitting) bias from Ch 2.
- **Chasing the fashionable concept**: Last year's hot approach may not fit you at all.

## Worked Example
Trend following's profile: study the largest historical moves and you'll find they trended persistently. The concept: enter on a confirmed breakout in the trend's direction, accept that ~55–60% of trades will be small losers, and hold the minority of winners for very large R-multiples. A trader who *needs to be right* (Ch 2) will hate this concept and quit; a trader who believes "the big money is in the big move" can trade it for years. Same rules, different fit — driven by belief.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start from a concept you understand and believe in.
2. Trend following is robust and suits most temperaments — but only if you accept a low win rate.
3. Every concept has a characteristic reliability/reward shape — match it to your psychology.
4. Neural nets and heavily optimized models carry the highest curve-fitting risk.
5. The concept must be *objectively measurable* (step 6 of the 12-step model).
