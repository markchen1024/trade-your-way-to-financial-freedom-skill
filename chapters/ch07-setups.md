# Chapter 7: Using Setups

## Core Idea
A *setup* is a condition that must be present before you'll consider an entry — the "stalking" stage that lowers your risk before you pull the trigger. Setups matter, but they (and entries) are far less important to results than most traders believe.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Four Phases of Entry**: Entry is a process, not a single instant — setup conditions, then a stalking/short-term setup, then the actual entry trigger, then the immediate risk (stop). Each phase narrows risk.
- **Setups as risk-lowering conditions**: A setup is a precondition met *before* the entry signal; it shortens your effective time frame to find a lower-risk entry point ("stalking").
- **Filters vs. setups**: A *filter* screens the universe continuously (e.g., "only trade stocks above their 200-day average"); a *setup* is a specific pre-entry condition. Too many filters = curve-fitting; setups should be conceptually justified, not data-mined.
- **Survey of common setup types** (with examples): failed-test setups (a failed test of a prior high/low), climax/reversal setups, retracement setups, time-based setups, and the setups embedded in well-known public systems.

## Key Concepts
- **Setup**: A condition that must be true before an entry is allowed.
- **Stalking**: Shortening your time frame to find a lower-risk entry once a setup exists.
- **Filter**: A continuous screen on the trading universe (distinct from a discrete setup).
- **Failed-test setup**: Price tests a previous high/low and fails — a common reversal trigger.

## Mental Models
- Treat setups as *permission to look*, not *permission to buy* — the entry trigger (Ch 8) still has to fire.
- Distinguish filters (always-on screens) from setups (discrete pre-conditions); don't pile on filters to make a backtest prettier.

## Anti-patterns
- **Over-filtering**: Stacking filters until the system perfectly fits history — pure degrees-of-freedom bias (Ch 2).
- **Treating setups as the edge**: Lavishing attention on setups while neglecting exits and position sizing — optimizing the wrong pillar.
- **Confusing a setup with an entry**: A setup is a condition; you still need a trigger.

## Worked Example
A **failed-test setup**: a stock has resistance at a prior high of $50. Price rallies to $50.20, fails to hold, and closes back below $50 — the test of the high *failed*. That failure is the setup (the precondition). The trader now "stalks": they wait for the actual entry trigger (Ch 8) — say a break below the failure-bar's low — and place a stop just above $50.20, defining a tight R. The setup didn't create the trade; it lowered the risk and told the trader where to look.

## Key Takeaways
1. A setup is a *pre-entry condition*, not the entry itself.
2. Setups exist to lower risk by "stalking" a better entry point.
3. Filters (continuous screens) ≠ setups (discrete conditions).
4. Don't over-filter — it's curve-fitting in disguise.
5. Setups and entries matter less than exits and position sizing.
