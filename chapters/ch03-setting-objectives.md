# Chapter 3: Setting Your Objectives

## Core Idea
Objectives are not an afterthought — they are a *major part of the system design itself*. You cannot build a system that fits you until you have precisely defined what you want, what you can tolerate, and what your constraints are.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Objectives-first system design**: Define objectives *before* selecting concepts, entries, or exits, because every later design choice (time frame, risk per trade, drawdown tolerance) is derived from them.
  - When to use: At the very start of building or overhauling any system.
  - How: Quantify goals as *gain wanted* and *drawdown tolerable*, then design backward from those numbers.
- **Tom Basso's objective-setting questions**: A practical interview with yourself covering capital, the return you *need* vs *want*, the drawdown you can psychologically and financially tolerate, and your time/effort constraints.

## Key Concepts
- **Required return**: How much you must make on trading capital just to survive (e.g., someone needing to make 30%+ per year just to live is in a very different position from someone who needs 0%). This number constrains how aggressive the system must be.
- **Drawdown tolerance**: The peak-to-trough equity decline you can endure *both* financially and emotionally without abandoning the system.
- **Acceptable risk per trade**: A direct input to position sizing (Ch 12).
- **Time frame**: How long you intend to hold and how much time you can devote — shapes everything downstream.

## Mental Models
- Think of objectives as the *specification* and the system as the *implementation*: most people skip the spec and wonder why nothing fits.
- Frame trading goals as a **risk/reward pair** ("I want X% return and can stomach a Y% drawdown"), never as a return number alone.

## Anti-patterns
- **Building a system, then asking what it's for**: Guarantees a mismatch between the system and the trader.
- **Stating a return goal with no drawdown constraint**: A 50%-return system you can't sit through is worthless because you'll abandon it at the worst moment.
- **Copying someone else's objectives**: Their capital, required return, and tolerance differ from yours.

## Worked Example
Two traders consider the *same* system. Trader A has $1,000,000 in investment capital but needs only ~$50,000/year to live (5% required return) — they can run a slow, low-drawdown system. Trader B has $100,000 and needs $30,000/year (30% required return) — the same gentle system can't possibly meet their needs, so they must either change objectives (lower the required draw, add capital) or accept a far more aggressive, higher-drawdown approach. Same system, opposite suitability — decided entirely by objectives.

## Key Takeaways
1. Objectives drive design; design them first and quantify them.
2. Always pair a return target with a maximum tolerable drawdown.
3. Know your *required* return (survival) separately from your *desired* return.
4. Honest self-assessment (capital, tolerance, time) precedes any concept selection.
5. A "good" system is only good *relative to your objectives*.
