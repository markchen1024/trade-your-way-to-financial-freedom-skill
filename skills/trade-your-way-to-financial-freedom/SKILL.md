## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

**The central thesis**: There is no magic system ("Holy Grail"). The edge is internal. Effort should be weighted **Psychology ≈ 60% > Position Sizing ≈ 30% > System/Entry ≈ 10%** — most traders invert this. Trading is "100% psychology."

**The Six Keys to Investment Success** (Ch 6) — what actually drives results:
1. **Reliability** — % of trades that win (hit rate)
2. **Relative size of wins vs. losses** — measured in **R-multiples**
3. **Cost** — commissions + slippage
4. **Opportunity** — how often you trade
5. **Capital** — account size
6. **Position-sizing model** — how much you trade

Keys **1–4 = expectancy**; keys **5–6 = position sizing**.

**R-multiple** (Ch 6, 9): Express every trade as a multiple of its **initial risk R** (R = entry − stop, × size). Risk $500, gain $1,000 → **+2R**; lose $500 → **−1R**. Evaluate everything in R, not dollars.

**Expectancy** (Ch 6): The **mean R-multiple** of your trade distribution = expected profit per dollar risked.
`Expectancy = (Win% × avg win in R) − (Loss% × avg loss in R)`. A 40%-win system with +5R/−1R = **+1.4R/trade** — a low win rate can be excellent.
**Growth ≈ Expectancy × Opportunity − Cost** (Ch 11).

**The Golden Rule of Trading** (Ch 9–10): **Cut losses short** (protective stops define R) and **let profits run** (trailing exits create big-R winners). Your edge comes from how you *exit*, not what you buy.

**Entry barely matters** (Ch 8): A **random (coin-flip) entry** + 3× volatility trailing stop + 1% position sizing on 10 markets made money on ~80% of runs (100% with sizing). Exits and sizing make the money — not entry.

**Four position-sizing models** (Ch 12): (1) one unit per fixed $, (2) equal value units, (3) **Percent Risk / CPR: units = equity × %risk ÷ R** (the professional default — *equates risk*), (4) Percent Volatility (size by ATR). Same system + different sizing = completely different results.

**The 12-Step System Development Model** (Ch 4): self-inventory → gather info → objectives → time frame → study best moves → define concept → stops + costs → profit exits + expectancy → seek big-R trades → position sizing → improve → worst-case rehearsal. Order = priority; entry is near last.

**Judgmental biases** (Ch 2) sabotage all of it — chief among them the **need to be right** (cuts winners, holds losers), the **Lotto bias** (entry obsession), and **degrees-of-freedom bias** (curve-fitting). Naming the bias is the first step to overriding it.
