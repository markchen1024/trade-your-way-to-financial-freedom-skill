# Chapter 4: Steps to Developing a System (The 12-Step Model)

## Core Idea
Tharp's 12-step model is the master checklist for building a trading system. It deliberately puts self-assessment and objectives first and entry near-last — the reverse of how most people work.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 12-Step System Development Model** — the spine of the entire book:
  1. **Take an inventory** — assess yourself: psychology, strengths, weaknesses, beliefs, resources.
  2. **Develop an open mind and gather market information** — study how markets actually move.
  3. **Determine your objectives** — what you want and can tolerate (Ch 3).
  4. **Determine your time frame for trading** — day, swing, long-term; matches your temperament.
  5. **Determine the best historical moves in that time frame and note what they have in common** — find the moves you want to catch.
  6. **Define the concept behind those moves and how to measure it objectively** — your edge/idea (Ch 5).
  7. **Add your stops and transaction costs** — define risk (R) and the cost of trading (Ch 9, Ch 11).
  8. **Add your profit exits and determine your expectancy** — how you take profits; compute expectancy (Ch 6, Ch 10).
  9. **Look for huge-reward (big-R) trades** — ensure the system can capture large multiples of risk.
  10. **Optimize with position sizing** — meet objectives via *how much* (Ch 12).
  11. **Determine how you can improve your system** — iterate.
  12. **Worst-case scenario / mental planning** — rehearse disasters before they happen.
  - When to use: Whenever designing, auditing, or rebuilding any system.
  - How: Work the steps *in order*; do not jump to entry (step 6) before steps 1–5.

## Key Concepts
- **Concept (step 6)**: The objectively measurable idea your system exploits (trend, reversion, etc.).
- **Expectancy (step 8)**: The average R you make per trade — computed only after stops and exits are defined.
- **Worst-case mental rehearsal (step 12)**: Pre-deciding your response to every disaster so you act, not freeze.

## Mental Models
- Read the step order as a priority statement: **You → objectives → time frame → concept → exits → expectancy → position sizing → entry**. Entry is one of the *last* and least important pieces.
- Treat the model as a loop, not a line — step 11 feeds back to the top.

## Anti-patterns
- **Starting at step 6 (the concept/entry)**: Skipping self-inventory and objectives produces a system you can't trade.
- **Skipping step 12**: Without rehearsed worst-case plans, panic dictates your decisions in a crisis.
- **Computing expectancy before defining exits**: Expectancy is meaningless without stops and profit-exits in place.

## Worked Example
Applying steps 5–8 to a trend system: you study the biggest moves in your chosen time frame (step 5) and notice they all begin after a breakout from a long base — that's your *concept* (step 6). You add a stop at 3× volatility below entry, defining your risk R, and include commissions/slippage (step 7). You add a trailing-stop profit exit (step 8) and backtest: average winner +5R, average loser −1R, win rate 40% → positive expectancy. Only now (step 10) do you layer a 1%-risk position-sizing model to hit your objectives.

## Key Takeaways
1. The 12 steps are an ordered checklist — order encodes priority.
2. Self-inventory and objectives come first; entry comes near last.
3. Expectancy (step 8) is computed *after* stops and exits exist.
4. Position sizing (step 10) is how you actually meet objectives.
5. Always finish with worst-case mental rehearsal (step 12).
