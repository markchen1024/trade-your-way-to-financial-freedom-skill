# Chapter 2: Judgmental Biases — Why Mastering the Markets Is So Difficult

## Core Idea
We trade our *beliefs* about the market, not the market itself — and a predictable set of cognitive biases distorts those beliefs at every stage. Awareness of the specific bias is the first step to neutralizing it.

## Frameworks Introduced
Tharp groups biases by *where in the process they strike*: building a system, testing a system, and trading a system.

### Biases that affect system development
- **Representation bias**: Assuming something *is* what it merely *represents* — e.g., treating a daily bar (a representation) as the actual price action.
- **Reliability bias**: Assuming data/indicators are accurate and complete when they aren't.
- **Lotto bias**: Wanting to *control* the market by focusing obsessively on entry (picking the number), because entry feels like the part you control — when exits and sizing matter far more.
- **Law-of-small-numbers bias**: Seeing patterns in tiny samples (a few trades "prove" a system). Combined with conservatism bias, it cements false beliefs.
- **Conservatism bias**: Once a belief is formed, failing to update it in the face of new contradicting evidence. *May be the root bias behind all others.*
- **Randomness bias**: Wanting to believe the market is orderly/predictable and refusing to accept it has large random components; also assuming markets aren't as random as they are.
- **Need-to-understand bias**: Imposing order and "reasons" on essentially random market data.

### Biases that affect testing
- **Degrees-of-freedom bias**: Over-optimizing — adding parameters/rules until the system fits historical data perfectly (curve-fitting) and fails live.
- **Postdictive error bias**: Accidentally using future information in a test (data that wouldn't have been available at the time), producing fantasy results.

### Biases that affect live trading
- **The need to be right**: The single most destructive obsession. It makes traders cut profits short (to lock in being "right") and let losses run (to avoid admitting being "wrong").
- **Gambler's fallacy bias**: After a run of losses, believing a win is "due" (and vice versa). A consequence of the randomness bias.
- **Protection bias / not protecting capital**: Failing to take small losses; the bias of not having (or honoring) a stop.

## Mental Models
- Treat every strong market opinion as a *belief to be tested*, not a fact — selective perception may have already discarded the most useful information.
- When you feel certain, suspect the **conservatism bias**; when you crave a reason, suspect the **need-to-understand bias**; when you can't take a loss, suspect the **need to be right**.

## Anti-patterns
- **Optimizing to a perfect backtest** (degrees-of-freedom): the more parameters you tune, the more you've memorized noise.
- **Cutting winners / holding losers**: the behavioral signature of the need to be right — it inverts the golden rule of trading.

## Worked Example
The need to be right in action: a trader buys a stock that drops. Admitting the loss means admitting being "wrong," so they hold (and even average down) hoping to get back to breakeven — letting a small loss become a large one. Meanwhile a winning trade is sold quickly to "bank a profit" and *be right*, capping the gain. Over many trades this produces the textbook losing distribution: small frequent wins and rare catastrophic losses — the exact opposite of what expectancy requires.

## Key Takeaways
1. You trade your beliefs; biased beliefs produce biased trading.
2. The most expensive bias in live trading is **the need to be right** — it sabotages exits.
3. Curve-fitting (degrees-of-freedom) and postdictive error make backtests lie.
4. Naming the specific bias as it arises is how you get the *ability to change* it.
5. Accepting market randomness is a prerequisite for sound system design.
